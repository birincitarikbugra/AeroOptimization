Aeroptimization

Air Vehicle Design and Development Project
Scheduling with Uncertain Activity Durations

The Problem

Aerospace development is messy in ways that the Critical Path Method was never designed to capture. Every task is treated as a fixed number of days, yet a single design review can stretch from twelve days to twenty four depending on change requests and reviewer availability. The method also points to one chain of tasks as critical, but in practice three different chains compete to become the longest, and watching only one means watching the wrong one much of the time.

The deepest issue is staffing. CPM assumes that engineers, reviewers and test rigs are always available. In a real programme they are not. When two critical tasks need the same team at once, one of them waits, and waiting accumulates. Across a programme of more than two hundred activities, these three blind spots quietly cost years.

Methods

CPM

The starting skeleton. It maps how tasks depend on one another and finds the longest chain through the network. This is our reference point.

PERT and Monte Carlo Simulation

Each task gets a range instead of a single number. Low risk tasks vary a little, test and certification tasks can stretch a lot. We run the project ten thousand times to see the spread of outcomes.

Risk Indices

For every task we measure three numbers: how often it lands on the critical path, how strongly its delay pulls the project finish, and how much variance it contributes overall. Together they rank the tasks that really drive the schedule.

Robust Scheduling

We compare every relaxation strategy across optimistic, baseline and pessimistic worlds, then pick the one whose worst case is still the best. The result holds up no matter how the future unfolds.

Results
CPM predicts 3.099 days. With uncertainty and limited staff modeled, the realistic figure at 80% confidence is 4.214 days. The hidden gap is about 1.115 days, roughly three years.

How the slack works. Each non critical task has a buffer called slack. If the delay is smaller than the slack, the project is not affected. If the delay exceeds the slack, the surplus pushes the project finish day for day. Critical tasks have zero slack, so every day of delay reaches the project.
